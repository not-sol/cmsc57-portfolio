Counting

Basics of Counting

Counting basics taught me to calculate possibilities in daily choices and games. It improved my logic for predictions and scheduling. This foundational topic helped me approach more complex problems by breaking them into structured, countable steps.

Pigeonhole Principle

The pigeonhole principle showed that duplication is unavoidable when space is limited. It applies in hashing, game uniqueness, and storage. I’ve used it to solve logic puzzles and understand real-world distribution challenges more clearly.

Permutation and Combination

I learned when order matters and how to count arrangements or selections. It’s useful in team setups, puzzles, and password logic. This topic improved my problem-solving by helping me explore possible outcomes efficiently.

Binomial Coefficients

Binomial coefficients helped me expand expressions and analyze probability scenarios. I applied them in games involving dice and chance. Pascal’s Triangle made the patterns clear, enhancing my grasp of probability and combinatorics.

Generalized Permutation and Computation

This topic showed me how to count with repetition or restrictions—like repeated digits in PINs. It’s useful in planning, inventory, and grouping problems with limited options. It helped me solve constrained scenarios logically.

Generating Permutations and Combinations

Learning to generate permutations and combinations taught me to explore all outcomes in puzzles, coding, and games. It’s useful for password cracking, quiz generation, and scheduling. This improved my logic by helping me systematically cover every possible case.

Graphs

Graphs and Graph Models

Graphs model real-world systems like social networks, maps, and internet structures. They help visualize and solve problems involving connections between entities, making them essential in computer science and data analysis.

Graph Terminology and Special Types of Graphs

Understanding graph terms and types—like trees, cycles, and complete graphs—helps identify structures and select appropriate algorithms. These concepts are key in organizing data and solving network-related problems.

Representing Graphs and Graph Isomorphism

Graphs can be represented using lists or matrices. Isomorphism shows when different-looking graphs are structurally the same. This helps simplify problems and recognize similar patterns in data and networks.

Connectivity

Connectivity measures how well nodes are linked. It ensures paths exist between points, crucial for networks, communication systems, and reliable infrastructure design in both real-world and digital environments.

Euler and Hamiltonian Paths

Euler paths visit edges once; Hamiltonian paths visit vertices once. These help in route optimization problems, such as circuit design, travel routes, and puzzle-solving strategies.

Shortest-Path Problem

This involves finding the quickest route between two points. Algorithms like Dijkstra’s are used in navigation systems, game AI, and network routing for efficient movement and data transfer.

Planar Graphs

Planar graphs can be drawn without crossing edges. They're used in map design, circuit layout, and reducing visual clutter in diagrams and network representations.

Graph Coloring

Graph coloring assigns colors to nodes without conflicts. It’s applied in scheduling, resource allocation, and frequency assignment to avoid overlap and manage constraints effectively.
